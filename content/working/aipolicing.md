abstract: Modern policing uses artificial intelligence (AI) tools for a variety of purposes including suspect identification, monitoring, and risk assessment. These tools are often promoted as being less biased as they use computational methods for policing tasks. However, given the documented racial biases in the algorithms and their applications, how do Americans actually view these tools? And do their evaluations differ depending on who promotes the use of AI in policing? We field two survey experiments that manipulate AI type, whether the AI sponsor is a government or private entity, and the race and gender of the sponsor to explore the contours of support for these tools by the American public. Surprisingly, we find that facial recognition receives more support than general AI tools, particularly among white and Republican respondents. We see little differences in support depending on whether the sponsor is a local police chief or a private entity. We also find some evidence that the race and gender of the sponsoring city councilperson matters for respondents' support of AI  in policing. This project has implications not only for our understanding of public perceptions of AI in policing, but also for accountability and representation in this  essential street-level bureaucracy.
